\begin{abstract}
  We introduce a new method for proving bilinear complexity lower bounds for matrix multiplication over finite fields. The approach combines the substitution method with a systematic backtracking search over linear restrictions on the first matrix $A$ in the product $AB = C^T$. We enumerate restriction classes up to symmetry; for each class we either obtain a rank lower bound by classical arguments or branch further via the substitution method. The search is organized by dynamic programming on the restricted matrix $A$. As an application we prove that the bilinear complexity of multiplying two $3 \times 3$ matrices over $\mathbb{F}_2$ is at least $20$, improving the longstanding lower bound of $19$ (Bl{\"a}ser 2003). The proof is found automatically within 1.5 hours on a laptop and verified in seconds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Previous Lower Bounds}

Denote $\langle l,m,n\rangle$ the matrix multiplication tensor between an $l\times m$ matrix and an $m \times n$ matrix, and $\mathbf{R}$ the tensor rank.

Winograd 1971 \cite{winograd1971multiplication} showed $\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,2,2\rangle)\ge 7$ over $\mathbb{Q}$.

Hopcroft and Kerr 1971 \cite{hopcroft1971minimizing} showed
$\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,2,n\rangle)\ge \lceil 7n/2\rceil$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$ and $\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,3,3\rangle)\ge 15$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$. They also mentioned $\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,3,4\rangle)\ge 19$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$, but without a formal proof.

Bl{\"a}ser 1999 \cite{blaser1999lower} showed
$\mathbf{R}(\langle l,m,n\rangle)\ge lm+mn+l-m+n-3$ for $n\ge l\ge 2$ over algebraically closed fields.
It implies the same lower bound over $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{F}_2$.

Bl{\"a}ser 1999 \cite{blaser1999fivehalf} showed
$\mathbf{R}(\langle n,n,n\rangle)\ge \frac{5}{2} n^2-3n$ over an arbitrary field.

Bl{\"a}ser 2003 \cite{blaser2003complexity} showed
$\mathbf{R}(\langle n,m,n\rangle)\ge 2mn+2n-m-2$ for $m\ge n\ge 3$ over an arbitrary field.

\section{Our Results}

We prove that the tensor rank of the matrix multiplication tensor for two $3 \times 3$ matrices is at least 20 over $\mathbb{F}_2$.
That is, any bilinear algorithm for multiplying two $3 \times 3$ matrices over $\mathbb{F}_2$ must use at least 20 scalar multiplications.
\begin{theorem}
  $\mathbf{R}(\langle 3,3,3\rangle)\ge 20$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.
\end{theorem}

This improves the previous lower bound of 19 by Bl{\"a}ser 2003 \cite{blaser2003complexity}.


We also complete the proof of $\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,3,4\rangle)\ge 19$, which was skipped in Hopcroft and Kerr 1971 \cite{hopcroft1971minimizing}.
\begin{theorem}
  $\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,3,4\rangle)\ge 19$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.
\end{theorem}

Futhermore, we reproduce the lower bounds for several other matrix sizes.

\section{Preliminaries}
\subsection{Symmetries}

The symmetries of the matrix multiplication tensor have been well studied~\cite{de1978varieties,chiantini2019geometry}.

Consider the matrix multiplication problem $A B = C^T$, where $A$ is an $l\times m$ matrix, $B$ is an $m\times n$ matrix, and $C$ is an $n\times l$ matrix.
The matrix multiplication tensor is
$$
  \sum_{i=0}^{l-1}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a_{i,j}\otimes b_{j,k}\otimes c_{k,i}.
$$

For any invertible matrices $P$, $Q$, and $R$, $(PAQ^{-1})(QBR^{-1}) = (RCP^{-1})^T$. The matrix multiplication tensor rank is preserved. We call this the \emph{sandwich symmetry}.

The next symmetry is the \emph{cyclic symmetry}:
$$
  \mathbf{R} \left( \sum_{i=0}^{l-1}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a_{i,j}\otimes b_{j,k}\otimes c_{k,i} \right) =
  \mathbf{R} \left( \sum_{i=0}^{l-1}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} b_{j,k}\otimes c_{k,i}\otimes a_{i,j} \right).
$$

For square matrices $l=m=n$, we can transpose the three matrices but swap two of them. $B^T A^T = C^T$. Together with the cyclic symmetry, we have
$$
  \mathbf{R} \left( \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a_{i,j}\otimes b_{j,k}\otimes c_{k,i} \right) =
  \mathbf{R} \left( \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a_{j,i}\otimes c_{i,k}\otimes b_{k,j} \right).
$$
We call it the \emph{transpose symmetry}.

\subsection{Restrictions}

We consider the matrix multiplication problem with linear restrictions on matrix $A$. For example, the constraints $a_{i,j}=a_{j,i}$ give a symmetric $A$, and the constraints $a_{0,j}=0$ reduce it to an $\langle l-1,m,n\rangle$ problem.

Some sets of restrictions are equivalent: they define the same subspace, or they lie in the same \emph{orbit} under the symmetries (sandwich, cyclic, transpose).
For example, $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{0,1}=0\}$ and $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}=0\}$ are equivalent because they define the same subspace.
The constraints $\{a_{0,j}=0 : 0\le j<m\}$ and $\{a_{1,j}=0 : 0\le j<m\}$ are in the same orbit under the sandwich symmetry, since we can permute the rows of $A$ by $P$.

Next, we aim to enumerate all distinct orbits of restrictions on $A$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.
For each orbit, we select a canonical representative.

We can determine whether two restriction sets lie in the same subspace by performing Gauss-Jordan elimination into reduced row echelon form (RREF).
RREF is also the lexicographically minimal form (possibly after reversing the order of rows or columns, depending on the chosen lexicographic order).

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \caption{Enumerate restriction orbits on $l\times m$ matrix $A$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$}
  \label{alg:enumerate-orbits-simple}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Ensure Returns \textit{orbits}, a lexicographically ordered list of canonical representatives.
    A canonical representative is a lexicographically ordered list of restrictions.
    Each restriction is a $l\times m$ matrix over $\mathbb{F}_2$, or a binary integer of $lm$ bits.
    \State \textit{orbits} $\gets$ empty list
    \State \Call{Enumerate}{empty list}
    \State \Return \textit{orbits}
    \Statex
    \Function{Enumerate}{\textit{restrictions}}
    \If{$|\textit{restrictions}| = l m$}
    \State \Return
    \EndIf
    \State $\textit{last} \gets \text{last element of \textit{restrictions}}$ (or $0$ if empty)
    \For{$\textit{restriction} = \textit{last}+1 \ \textbf{to} \ 2^{lm}-1$}
    \If{\textit{restriction} is in subspace of \textit{restrictions}}
    \State \textbf{continue}
    \EndIf
    \State append \textit{restriction} to \textit{restrictions}
    \If{\Call{IsCanonical}{\textit{restrictions}}}
    \State append \textit{restrictions} to \textit{orbits}
    \State \Call{Enumerate}{\textit{restrictions}}
    \EndIf
    \State remove last element from \textit{restrictions}
    \EndFor
    \EndFunction
    \Statex
    \Function{IsCanonical}{\textit{restrictions}}
    \For{each full-rank matrix $P$ and $Q^{-1}$ in random order}
    \For{\textit{transpose} in \{\textbf{false}, \textbf{true}\} if $l=m=n$}
    \State $\textit{new\_restrictions} \gets \text{RREF of } \{ P M Q^{-1} : M \in \textit{restrictions} \}$ (transpose $M$ if \textit{transpose})
    \If{$\textit{new\_restrictions} <_{\mathrm{lex}} \textit{restrictions}$}
    \State \Return \textbf{false}
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \EndFor
    \State \Return \textbf{true}
    \EndFunction
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:enumerate-orbits-simple} finds 10 orbits in the $\langle 2,2,2\rangle$ case and 496 orbits in the $\langle 3,3,3\rangle$ case within a minute.


\section{A Toy Example: $\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,2,2\rangle)\ge 7$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$}

We take $\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,2,2\rangle)$ as an example to show how the tensor rank lower bound techniques work.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:enumerate-orbits-simple} found 10 orbits of restrictions on $A$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$ as below.


\subsection*{Orbit 0: $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{0,1}=0,\ a_{1,0}=0,\ a_{1,1}=0\}$}
$A=\begin{pmatrix}
    0 & 0 \\
    0 & 0
  \end{pmatrix}$.
The matrix multiplication tensor is $T_0=0$.
$\mathbf{R} (T_0) = 0$.

\subsection*{Orbit 1: $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{0,1}=0,\ a_{1,0}=0\}$}
$A=\begin{pmatrix}
    0 & 0       \\
    0 & a_{1,1}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
The matrix multiplication tensor is $T_1 = a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$.

\begin{lemma}[Flattening]\label{lem:flatten-rank}
  The rank of a trilinear tensor is greater than or equal to the matrix rank after flattening two dimensions.
\end{lemma}

In this case, we flatten $a$ and $b$ into one dimension $x$, and get $x_{1,1,1,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + x_{1,1,1,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$. It has matrix rank 2.
So, $\mathbf{R} (T_1) \geq 2$.

\subsection*{Orbit 2: $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0,\ a_{1,1}=0\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0       & a_{0,1} \\
    a_{0,1} & 0
  \end{pmatrix}$.
The matrix multiplication tensor is $T_2 = a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,1} \otimes c_{1,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,0} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,0}$.
By Flattening Lemma (Lemma~\ref{lem:flatten-rank}), we flatten $a$ and $b$ into one dimension $x$, and get $x_{0,1,0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + x_{0,1,0,1} \otimes c_{1,1} + x_{0,1,1,0} \otimes c_{0,0} + x_{0,1,1,1} \otimes c_{1,0}$. It has matrix rank 4.
So, $\mathbf{R} (T_2) \geq 4$.

\subsection*{Orbit 3: $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{0,1}=0\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0       & 0       \\
    a_{1,0} & a_{1,1}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
The matrix multiplication tensor is $T_3 = a_{1,0} \otimes b_{0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{1,0} \otimes b_{0,1} \otimes c_{1,1} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$.
By Flattening Lemma (Lemma~\ref{lem:flatten-rank}), we flatten $a$ and $b$ into one dimension $x$, and get $x_{1,0,0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + x_{1,0,0,1} \otimes c_{1,1} + x_{1,1,1,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + x_{1,1,1,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$. It has matrix rank 4.
So, $\mathbf{R} (T_3) \geq 4$.

\subsection*{Orbit 4: $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0       & a_{0,1} \\
    a_{0,1} & a_{1,1}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
The matrix multiplication tensor is $T_4 = a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,1} \otimes c_{1,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,0} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,0} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$.

For any orbit, the Flattening Lemma (Lemma~\ref{lem:flatten-rank}) can only give a lower bound at most 4.
In this orbit, we need another technique.

\begin{lemma}[Lemma 2 in \cite{hopcroft1971minimizing}]\label{lem:lemma-2}
  Let $F=\{f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_{s-1}, \ldots, f_{t-1}\}$ be a set of expressions, where $f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_{s-1}$ are independent, and each can be expressed as a single product.
  If $F$ can be computed with $p$ multiplications, then there exists an algorithm for $F$ with $p$ multiplications in which $s$ of the multiplications are $f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_{s-1}$.
\end{lemma}

Choose $F=\{a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0}, a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1}\}$. $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0}$ and $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1}$ are independent, and single products.
By Lemma 2, we can assume the decomposition scheme contains $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes (c_{0,0}+[c_{0,1}]+[c_{1,1}])+$ and $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes (c_{1,0}+[c_{0,1}]+[c_{1,1}])$, where $[x]$ means $x$ may or may not be present.
After removing the two terms from the decomposition scheme, the remaining tensor is $T_4 + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes (c_{0,0}+[c_{0,1}]+[c_{1,1}]) + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes (c_{1,0}+[c_{0,1}]+[c_{1,1}])$. By enumerating all the $2^4=16$ cases of the presence of the 4 square-bracket terms, we find that all of them have rank 4 by the Flattening Lemma (Lemma~\ref{lem:flatten-rank}).
So, $\mathbf{R} (T_4) \geq 4+2=6$.

\subsection*{Orbit 5: $\{a_{0,0}=0,\ a_{1,1}=0\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0       & a_{0,1} \\
    a_{1,0} & 0
  \end{pmatrix}$.
The matrix multiplication tensor is $T_5 = a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,0} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,0} + a_{1,0} \otimes b_{0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{1,0} \otimes b_{0,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$.
By Flattening Lemma (Lemma~\ref{lem:flatten-rank}), we flatten $a$ and $b$ into one dimension $x$, and get $x_{0,1,1,0} \otimes c_{0,0} + x_{0,1,1,1} \otimes c_{1,0} + x_{1,0,0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + x_{1,0,0,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$. It has matrix rank 4.
So, $\mathbf{R} (T_5) \geq 4$.

\subsection*{Orbit 6: $\{a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0,\ a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}+a_{1,1}=0\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{0,0} & a_{0,1}         \\
    a_{0,1} & a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
The matrix multiplication tensor is $T_6=a_{0,0} \otimes b_{0,0} \otimes c_{0,0} + a_{0,0} \otimes b_{0,1} \otimes c_{1,0} + a_{0,0} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{0,0} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,1} \otimes c_{1,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,0} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,1} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,0} + a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,1}$.

First, we have $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 4$ by flattening the tensor. Then, we apply the substitution method on $A$.

If $a_{0,0}$ appears at least twice in the decomposition scheme, we set $a_{0,0}=0$. Then,
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0       & a_{0,1} \\
    a_{0,1} & 0
  \end{pmatrix}$.
It is in Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$.

If $a_{0,1}$ appears at least twice in the decomposition scheme, we set $a_{0,1}=0$. Then,
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{0,0} & 0       \\
    0       & a_{0,0}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
It is in Orbit 5 by swapping two rows and two columns of $A$. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$.

If $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$ appears at least twice in the decomposition scheme, we set $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}=0$. Then,
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{0,0} & a_{0,0} \\
    a_{0,0} & 0
  \end{pmatrix}$.
It is in Orbit 5 by adding the second row to the first row. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$.

Because $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 4$, at least one of the three cases must hold. Hence $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$.

\subsection*{Orbit 7: $\{a_{0,0}=0\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0       & a_{0,1} \\
    a_{1,0} & a_{1,1}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
We can reduce it to Orbit 4 by adding the restriction $a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0$. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_7) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_4) \geq 6$.

\subsection*{Orbit 8: $\{a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{0,0} & a_{0,1} \\
    a_{0,1} & a_{1,1}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
We can reduce it to Orbit 4 by adding a restriction $a_{0,0}=0$. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_8) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_4) \geq 6$.

\subsection*{Orbit 9: $\{\}$}
$A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{0,0} & a_{0,1} \\
    a_{0,1} & a_{1,1}
  \end{pmatrix}$.
This is the general $2\times 2$ case. $T_9 = \sum_{i=0}^{1}\sum_{j=0}^{1}\sum_{k=0}^{1} a_{i,j}\otimes b_{j,k}\otimes c_{k,i}$.

First, it is easy to show that $\mathbf{R} (T_9) \geq 1$. Then, we apply the substitution method on $A$.

If $a_{0,0}$ appears at least once in the decomposition scheme, we set $a_{0,0}=0$.
Then, we reduce it to Orbit 7. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_9) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_7)+1 \geq 6+1=7$.
The same argument applies to $a_{0,1}$, $a_{1,0}$, $a_{1,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$, $a_{1,0}+a_{1,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{1,0}$, $a_{0,1}+a_{1,1}$, and $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}+a_{1,1}$ by the sandwich symmetry.

If $a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}$ appears at least once in the decomposition scheme, we set $a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 8. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_9) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_8)+1 \geq 6+1=7$. The same argument applies to $a_{0,0}+a_{1,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}+a_{1,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{1,0}+a_{1,1}$, $a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}+a_{1,1}$ by the sandwich symmetry.

We list all the possible restrictions on $A$. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_9) \geq 7$.

\section{Rank Lower Bound Techniques}

In this section, we formally describe the four techniques used in the toy example.

\subsection{Flattening}

Flatten dimensions $a$ and $b$, flatten dimensions $b$ and $c$, and flatten dimensions $c$ and $a$. Then, take the maximum of the three matrix ranks.

\subsection{Forced Product}

Example: Orbit 4.

Group the tensor by $c$ into $t$ terms. Example:
$T_4 =
  (a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,0} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,0}) \otimes c_{0,1} +
  (a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,1} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,1}) \otimes c_{1,1} +
  a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0} \otimes c_{0,0} +
  a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1} \otimes c_{1,0}$. $t=4$

Pick the terms that have matrix rank 1 on $a$ and $b$, i.e., those that can be expressed as a single product.
For example, the first term $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,0} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,0}$ has rank 2;
the second term $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{0,1} + a_{1,1} \otimes b_{1,1}$ has rank 2;
the third term $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0}$ has rank 1;
and the fourth term $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1}$ has rank 1.
So, we pick the third and fourth terms: $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,0}$ and $a_{0,1} \otimes b_{1,1}$.

Greedily choose the largest linearly independent subset of the picked terms of rank 1. Denote the subset size by $s$. In this example, the two terms are linearly independent, and we choose both.

Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:lemma-2} by setting $f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_{s-1}$ to the subset of size $s$ we just chose.
They are independent, and each can be expressed as a single product.
So, we can assume the decomposition scheme contains the $s$ terms.
After removing the $s$ terms from the decomposition scheme, we compute the rank of the remaining tensor. There are $s(t-s)$ terms which may or may not be present.
We enumerate all the $2^{s(t-s)}$ cases of the presence of the $s(t-s)$ terms, and compute the rank lower bound for each case by Flattening Technique, and take the minimum.
In practice, if $s(t-s) \ge 32$, we skip this technique because it runs too slowly.

Finally, we repeat the whole process on the three cyclic permutations of the tensor, and take the maximum of the three ranks.

\subsection{Degenerate Reduction}

Example: Orbit 7.

If one restriction set $X$ is contained in another restriction set $Y$, then the tensor rank of $T_X$ is at least the tensor rank of $T_Y$.

\subsection{Backtracking}

For Orbit 6, the rank lower bound proof was constructed by hand and is concise. In this section, we present an automated approach to generating the proof, which, while longer, is found systematically.

First, we use the previous three techniques to obtain a rank lower bound, denoted by \textit{known\_lower\_bound}.
Then we enumerate all possible factors of $A$, up to \textit{known\_lower\_bound} factors.
In each case we set a subset of the factors to zero and reduce to another orbit.

In the example of Orbit 6, there are $2^4-1$ possible non-zero factors of $A$:
$[a_{0,0}] + [a_{0,1}] + [a_{1,0}] + [a_{1,1}]$ except for 0.
Given the restriction set $\{a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0,\ a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}+a_{1,1}=0\}$, many factors are redundant.
For example, $a_{0,1}$ and $a_{1,0}$ are equal because $a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0$.
We pick a canonical factor (the lexicographically minimum one) among each set of equal factors.
The minimum factor should not contain $a_{i,j}$ if $(i,j)$ is the most significant bit of a restriction in \textit{restriction\_set}.
In the example, $(1,0)$ is the most significant bit of the restriction $a_{0,1}+a_{1,0}=0$, and $(1,1)$ is the most significant bit of the restriction $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}+a_{1,1}=0$.
So, the canonical factor should not contain $a_{1,0}$ or $a_{1,1}$.
It is sum of a non-empty subset of the others: $\{a_{0,0},\ a_{0,1}\}$, i.e.
the canonical factors are $a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,1}$, and $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \caption{Canonical Factors Enumeration}
  \label{alg:canonical-factors-enumeration}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require \textit{restriction\_set}, a set of restrictions on $A$, in RREF form.
    \Ensure Returns \textit{canonical\_factors}, a list of canonical factors on $A$.
    \State \textit{canonical\_factors} $\gets$ empty list
    \For{$\textit{factor} = 1$ to $2^{lm}-1$}
    \For{$\textit{restriction} \in \textit{restriction\_set}$}
    \If{\textit{factor} xor \textit{restriction} $<$ \textit{factor}}
    \State \textbf{continue} \textit{factor}-for-loop \Comment{The factor is not canonical}
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State append \textit{factor} to \textit{canonical\_factors}
    \EndFor
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

We have $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 4$ by the Flattening Technique.
Then, we enumerate factors in $\{a_{0,0}, a_{0,1}, a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}\}^4$.
As the factors are unordered, we assume the factors are non-decreasing.

We are listing the running progress of the backtracking algorithm.

$a_{0,0}$: We do not have a proof for $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$. Go deeper.

$a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,0}$: Set $a_{0,0}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$. Proved. We do not need to go deeper.

$a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,1}$: We do not have a proof for $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$. Go deeper.

$a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,1}$: Set $a_{0,1}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$. Proved. We do not need to go deeper.

$a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: We do not have a proof for $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$. Go deeper.

$a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: Set $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$. Proved. We do not need to go deeper.

$a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: We do not have a proof for $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$. Go deeper.
$a_{0,0}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: Set $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$. Proved. We do not need to go deeper.

$a_{0,1}$: We do not have a proof for $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$. Go deeper.

$a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,1}$: Set $a_{0,1}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$. Proved. We do not need to go deeper.

$a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: We do not have a proof for $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$. Go deeper.

$a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: Set $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$. Proved. We do not need to go deeper.

$a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: We do not have a proof for $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq 6$. Go deeper.

$a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$, $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}$: Set $a_{0,0}+a_{0,1}=0$. Then, we reduce it to Orbit 5. So, $\mathbf{R} (T_6) \geq \mathbf{R} (T_5) +2 \geq 4+2=6$. Proved. We do not need to go deeper.

Formally, we have the following algorithm:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \caption{Backtracking Algorithm}
  \label{alg:backtracking}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require \textit{restriction\_set}, a set of restrictions on $A$, in RREF form.
    \Require the best-known rank lower bound of each orbit.
    \Require the target lower bound
    \Ensure Returns the rank lower bound with a \textit{proof}
    \State Compute \textit{canonical\_factors}
    \Function{Backtrack}{\textit{factors}}
    \If{\textit{factors} is not empty}
    \For{each sub-list \textit{sub\_factors} of \textit{factors} containing the last factor}
    \State \textit{extended\_restrictions} $\gets$ \textit{restriction\_set} + \textit{sub\_factors}
    \State Find the orbit of \textit{extended\_restrictions}, by enumerating all the possible symmetries followed by Gauss-Jordan elimination \label{alg:find-orbit}
    \If{$|\textit{sub\_factors}|$ +   best-known rank lower bound of  orbit of \textit{extended\_restrictions} $\geq$ the target lower bound}
    \State Save $|\textit{sub\_factors}|$ and the symmetry to  \textit{proof}
    \State \Return True \label{alg:find-a-proof}
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \EndIf
    \State \textit{last\_factor} $\gets$ the last element of \textit{factors}, or $2^{lm}-1$ if \textit{factors} is empty
    \For{\textit{factor} in \textit{canonical\_factors} and \textit{factor} $\leq$ \textit{last\_factor}}
    \If{\Call{Backtrack}{\textit{factors} + [\textit{factor}]} is False}
    \State \Return False
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \Return True
    \EndFunction
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This algorithm works for $\langle 2,2,2\rangle$, but it is too slow for larger matrices. We need to optimize it.

\subsubsection{Optimization: Multi-threading}
We parallelize all the orbits with the same number of restrictions, because the backtracking for an orbit depends on the other orbits with more restrictions.
After processing all orbits with a given number of restrictions, we proceed to orbits with one fewer restriction, until we reach the orbits with no restrictions (the general case).

Also, we parallelize the \textit{factor} for-loop in the first layer of the backtracking function.

\subsubsection{Optimization: Restriction Map}
Line~\ref{alg:find-orbit} in Algorithm~\ref{alg:backtracking} needs to enumerate all the $l \times l$ full-rank matrices $P$, all the $m \times m$ full-rank matrices $Q^{-1}$, and whether to transpose.
It is too slow.
To get a trade-off between speed and memory usage, we can use a hash map that stores, for each orbit, the pairs $(Q^{-1}, \textit{transpose})$.
When looking up, we only enumerate over $P$.
This makes the lookup time roughly a square root faster.

To improve the inserting speed, we use sharding. We split the hash map into multiple shards (e.g. 997 shards), each one has a mutex lock, and insert the elements in parallel.

\subsubsection{Optimization: Local Map and Memory Management}
The restriction map is faster, but it still needs to enumerate all the $P$s.
So, we build a local cache in each thread for each orbit: a hash map from \textit{sub\_factors} to the lower bound for that sub-list.
In some cases, the local cache still grows too large.
So, when the local cache is getting larger than some threshold, we randomly delete half of the elements to avoid OOM.
In practice, the threshold is set to $10^7$.

\subsubsection{Optimization: Incremental Target Lower Bound}
We set the target lower bound to the best-known rank lower bound plus one.
So, it improves the rank lower bound one at a time.

\subsubsection{Optimization: Step Limit}
In some cases, the backtracking algorithm runs too slowly.
So, we limit the number of steps.
When it reaches the limit, we cancel the search.


\section{Proof and Verification}

For each orbit we record which technique was used in the proof.

If the Forced Product technique is used, we save the cyclic permutation type (a number in $\{0,1,2\}$) in the proof.

If the Degenerate Reduction technique is used, we save the extra restriction $Y \setminus X$ in the proof.

If the Backtracking technique is used, the proof can be very long.
To achieve a trade-off between proof size and verification cost, we save the following at Line~\ref{alg:find-a-proof} in Algorithm~\ref{alg:backtracking}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the size of \textit{factors};
  \item the index set of \textit{sub\_factors} within \textit{factors};
  \item the symmetry (the matrices $P$, $Q^{-1}$, and whether to transpose).
\end{itemize}

\section{Applications}

\subsection{$\mathbf{R}(\langle 3,3,3\rangle)\ge 20$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$}
With step limit $10^7$, our program takes 71 minutes to find the proof on a MacBook Air M4 with 8 cores and 16 GB memory.

The proof is about 32\,MiB. It can be verified on the same computer within 10 seconds.


\subsection{$\mathbf{R}(\langle 2,3,4\rangle)\ge 19$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$}
Our program actually proves $\mathbf{R}(\langle 3,2,4\rangle)\ge 19$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.
The step limit is $10^7$.
It takes 110 minutes to find the proof on a MacBook Air M4 with 8 cores and 16 GB memory.

The proof is under 100\,KiB and can be verified on the same computer in 110 minutes. Although verification takes as long as the search, it remains useful: the verifier program is shorter than the search program and is therefore easier to audit for correctness.
